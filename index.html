New parent reference guide

Your first weeks home with baby

A calm, practical reference for the whole family. You've got this, Mom.

The daily rhythm (weeks 1–4)
There is no real "schedule" yet — newborns eat, sleep, and repeat every 2–3 hours around the clock. Your job is to respond, not impose a routine.

Daytime naps — you don't have to stay in the bedroom
Baby comes with you — you are not sequestered to the bedroom during naps. No monitor needed when you're in the same room. A monitor becomes relevant later when baby moves to their own nursery.
Move it
Wheel the bassinet to wherever you are. Many bassinets are on wheels for exactly this reason — living room, kitchen, wherever.
Second spot
Keep a pack-n-play in your main living area. A dedicated daytime nap spot means you never have to move the bassinet back and forth.
Babywear
Use a soft carrier or wrap. Baby sleeps on you, hands-free. Follow safe babywearing: face always visible, chin off chest, airways clear.
Hold them
Contact naps are completely fine in the early weeks. Not a bad habit yet. The closeness is good for both of you.
Your role as support partner
  • Take shifts — one parent "on duty" while the other sleeps
  • Handle burping, diapering, and resettling after feeds so your wife can rest
  • Keep a simple log: feeding time, duration, last diaper — helps spot patterns fast
  • Eat, drink water, and sleep whenever baby sleeps
Feeding
Breastfeeding basics
1
Latch is everything. Baby's mouth should cover the areola, not just the nipple. Lips flanged out. A good latch shouldn't hurt — initial tenderness is normal, sharp pain is not.
2
Feed on demand, both breasts. Offer whenever baby shows hunger cues. Switch sides each session. Start on the side you left off.
3
Track output to track intake. Count wet/dirty diapers. 6+ wet diapers/day after day 4 = adequate intake.
4
Engorgement is common. Warm compress before feeding, cold pack after. A lactation consultant can help with most issues.
Bottle feeding (formula or pumped milk)
1
Preparation. Formula: mix exactly as directed. Pumped milk: warm gently in warm water bath, never microwave.
2
Paced feeding. Hold bottle horizontal. Let baby control the flow. Take breaks. Prevents overfeeding and gas.
3
Amount guide. First days: 1–2 oz. End of week 1: 1.5–3 oz. Weeks 2–4: 2–4 oz. Feed until baby shows fullness cues.
4
Storage. Formula: toss after 1 hour at room temp. Breastmilk: 4 hrs room temp, 4 days fridge, 6 months freezer.
Burping — every time
  • When: midway through feed and at the end
  • Over shoulder: upright against chest, gently pat/rub back
  • Sitting upright: support chin, lean slightly forward, pat back
  • Across lap: face down on thighs, support head, pat back
  • Burp for 2–3 minutes — no burp is okay, try upright time instead
  • Some spit-up is normal. "Happy spitter" = thriving despite spitting up
Pumping & bottles — how it all fits together
The goal in the early weeks is to establish and protect milk supply. Milk needs to be removed — by nursing or pumping — roughly every 2–3 hours. Long gaps send a signal to produce less.
The three modes
Direct nursingThe foundation
The default, whenever possible. Baby is better at extracting milk than any pump, and the hormonal feedback loop works best with direct feeding. Building supply starts here. Do this as the primary method for all feeds you can.
PumpingBuilds supply & creates a stash
Pump to build a freezer stash, relieve engorgement, maintain supply when baby can't nurse, or create bottles for a partner to use. Pumping after a nursing session in the early weeks (even if little comes out) signals the body to produce more. Best time to pump: right after the first morning feed — supply is highest then.
Bottle of pumped milkBuys a sleep stretch
Lets your wife sleep through one overnight feed while you handle it, or lets other family members feed baby. The key rule: if she skips a nursing session, she should pump at roughly the same time — otherwise supply takes a hit over time.
The golden rule: skip a feed = pump at the same time
Wife skips overnight feed → You bottle-feed baby → Wife pumps same time
She keeps supply up, you both handle the feed, she gets a longer sleep stretch.
Wife skips feed, no pump → Supply starts to dip
Okay occasionally, not as a pattern in early weeks.
A realistic daily pumping rhythm (weeks 1–4)
Morning
Best pump session of the day. Pump once after the first morning nursing session. Supply is highest and letdown is easiest. This is your primary stash-building session.
Daytime
Nurse directly as much as possible. Pump only if engorged, if baby skipped a feed, or if you're trying to boost supply.
Evening
Optional second pump. Some families pump once in the evening to build the overnight bottle supply. Worth doing if supply feels low or you want a bigger stash.
Overnight
One partner does a bottle feed, the other pumps. Wife pumps at the same time as the bottle feed — ideally the 2–3am session. Pump and go right back to sleep. Protects supply while giving her a longer stretch.
Pumping practicalities
  • Flange size matters more than people realize. The nipple should move freely in the tunnel, not the areola. Wrong size = pain, reduced output, and damaged tissue. Most people need a different size than what comes in the box.
  • Output varies widely and that's normal. 0.5–2 oz total per session is common in early weeks, even with plentiful supply. Baby is more efficient than the pump.
  • Wash pump parts after every use — or keep them in a sealed bag in the fridge between sessions (up to 24 hrs) to save time overnight.
  • Hospital-grade pumps are more powerful and can be rented. Worth it in the early weeks especially if trying to establish supply or baby has trouble nursing.
  • Hands-free pumping bras are life-changing. Pump and do something else at the same time.
  • Stay hydrated while pumping. Keep a large water bottle next to the pump at all times.
Introducing the bottle — timing matters
  • Wait until breastfeeding is well-established before introducing a bottle — usually around 3–4 weeks. Too early can cause "nipple confusion" and latch issues.
  • For late preterm babies: your pediatrician or lactation consultant may recommend introducing a bottle sooner if baby is struggling to get enough milk at the breast.
  • Have someone other than your wife give the first few bottles — baby may refuse from mom since they know the real thing is right there.
  • Use a slow-flow nipple (size 0 or 1) and pace the feed — hold bottle horizontal, let baby pause and control the flow.
  • If baby refuses the bottle, try different brands/nipple shapes, different temperatures of milk, different holders, or offering when baby is calm but not starving.

Diapering
Diaper change — step by step
1
Gather everything first. Clean diaper, wipes, cream if needed. Never leave baby unattended on the changing table.
2
Open dirty diaper. Use the front to wipe the biggest mess forward, fold it under. For boys: drape a wipe over the penis immediately.
3
Wipe thoroughly. Girls: always front to back. Boys: wipe under the scrotum. Get into all the creases.
4
Lift and slide. Grab both ankles gently, lift bottom just enough to swap diapers. Tabs go in the back.
5
Fasten snugly. Two fingers should fit inside the waistband. Fold below the umbilical stump until it falls off.
6
Apply cream if needed. Thin zinc oxide layer for redness. Barrier cream at each change if prone to rash.
Nighttime diaper changes: Do everything in your bedroom — no need to go to the nursery. Keep a changing pad and supplies on your dresser or in a basket by the bassinet. Change first, then feed. Keep lights dim, voices quiet, interaction minimal.
What's normal in the diaper
Day 1–2: meconium (black/tarry) Day 3–4: transitional (green-brown) Day 5+: seedy yellow (breastfed) Formula: tan/yellow-green
  • Breastfed poop ranges from mustard yellow to green — very normal
  • Formula-fed poop is thicker and darker
  • Newborns may poop after every feed or go several days — both can be normal
  • Red blood, white/chalky, or black after meconium phase = call pediatrician
Umbilical cord stump care
  • Keep it clean and dry — fold diaper waistband below it
  • Sponge baths only until it falls off (1–3 weeks)
  • Don't pull it off — it falls off on its own
  • A little dried blood when it falls off is normal
  • Call the doctor if: base is red and warm, yellow discharge with odor, or still on after 3 weeks
Sleep & soothing
Safe sleep — non-negotiable rules
  • Back to sleep, every time — for naps and night
  • Firm, flat surface — crib or bassinet with firm mattress and tight-fitting sheet only
  • Nothing in the sleep space — no pillows, blankets, bumpers, or toys
  • Room sharing is great — bassinet next to your bed. Avoid bed-sharing.
  • Room temperature: 68–72°F
  • Pacifier at sleep time can help — offer after breastfeeding is established (~3–4 weeks)
The 5 S's — soothing a crying baby
1
Swaddle. Snug wrap, arms in. Calms the startle reflex. Stop at ~2 months or when rolling begins.
2
Side/Stomach position (in arms). Hold on their side against your chest. Always back for sleep.
3
Shush. Loud white noise — louder than you'd think. Right next to their ear or use a machine.
4
Swing. Gentle rhythmic motion — rock, bounce, sway. Support the head.
5
Suck. Breastfeeding, clean finger, or pacifier. Deep sucking is calming.
Why is baby crying? Quick checklist
  • Hungry? (most common) — try feeding even if recently fed
  • Diaper needs changing?
  • Too hot or cold? (check back of neck)
  • Overtired?
  • Gassy? Try bicycle legs, gentle clockwise tummy massage
  • Needs to be held?
  • Overstimulated? Try dimmer light, quiet, swaddle
  • Inconsolable for >2 hours or something seems off — call the pediatrician
Bathing & general care
Sponge bath (until cord falls off)
1
Gather everything first. Warm water (~100°F), soft washcloth, gentle baby soap, towel. Keep baby warm — expose only the area you're washing.
2
Face first, no soap. Wipe eyes inner to outer corner. Nose and ears: wipe exterior only.
3
Move down the body. Neck folds, armpits, chest, belly, arms, hands. Keep the cord stump dry.
4
Genitals and bottom. Front to back for girls. Uncircumcised boys: don't retract foreskin. Circumcised: follow hospital instructions.
5
Dry thoroughly. Pat dry, especially skin folds. No talcum powder.
Common newborn skin things (usually normal)
  • Milia: tiny white bumps on nose/chin — resolve on their own
  • Baby acne: peaks around 3–4 weeks, clears itself
  • Peeling skin: normal first 1–2 weeks, especially hands and feet
  • Jaundice: yellowish tint to skin/eyes — common, monitor and report to pediatrician
  • Cradle cap: flaky scalp around 2–3 weeks — gentle oil and soft brush helps
Taking temperature
  • Rectal thermometer is most accurate for newborns
  • Coat tip with petroleum jelly, insert ½ inch, hold 60 seconds
  • Normal: 97–100.4°F (36.1–38°C)
  • Fever in baby under 3 months (>100.4°F) = call doctor immediately
Postpartum care (for your wife)
This section is for your wife's recovery. The first 6 weeks postpartum are a major physical healing period — just as important as caring for baby.
Physical recovery — what to expect
  • Lochia (postpartum bleeding): Normal for up to 6 weeks. Starts heavy and red, lightens gradually. Soaking a pad in under an hour or passing large clots = call OB.
  • Perineal soreness: Ice packs first 24hrs, then sitz baths 2–3x daily. Peri bottle with warm water after each bathroom use. Witch hazel pads help swelling.
  • C-section incision: Keep dry and clean. No lifting heavier than baby. Watch for redness, swelling, or discharge.
  • Afterpains: Uterine cramping, especially during breastfeeding. Worst days 1–3. Ibuprofen (if cleared by OB) helps.
  • Swelling: Legs and feet may be puffier than during pregnancy at first. Elevate feet when possible.
  • Night sweats: Very common as hormones shift. Keep a towel nearby.
  • Constipation: Stool softeners often prescribed. Stay hydrated, eat fiber. Don't strain.
Breastfeeding — troubleshooting & support
1
Milk "coming in." Colostrum is produced from birth. Full milk typically comes in days 2–4. Breasts may feel very full and hard — engorgement eases within a few days.
2
Sore nipples. Tenderness in week 1 is common. Persistent sharp pain = latch issue. Apply expressed breastmilk to nipples after feeding — it heals. Lanolin cream also helps.
3
Blocked ducts. Hard, tender lump in the breast. Keep feeding on that side, massage toward nipple during feeds, warm compress before feeding. If doesn't resolve in 24–48hrs or you develop fever, call OB.
4
Mastitis. Flu-like symptoms + a red, hot, painful area on the breast. Needs antibiotics — call OB promptly. Keep feeding — stopping makes it worse.
5
Low supply concerns. Frequent feeding builds supply. Pump after feeds if needed. Stay very hydrated and eat enough (breastfeeding burns ~500 extra calories/day). A lactation consultant can assess and help.
6
Pumping basics. Aim for 8–10 times/day in early weeks to build supply. Flange size matters — ill-fitting flanges cause pain and reduce output. Hospital-grade pumps are available to rent.
Mental health — please read this
  • Baby blues: Mood swings, tearfulness, anxiety in first 1–2 weeks. Affects up to 80% of new mothers. Usually resolves on its own.
  • Postpartum depression (PPD): Persistent sadness, numbness, or disconnection beyond 2 weeks — or severe at any point. Very treatable. She should tell her OB.
  • Postpartum anxiety: Racing thoughts, constant worry, inability to rest. Also very treatable.
  • Postpartum psychosis (rare but urgent): Hallucinations, confusion, extreme behavior changes — seek emergency help immediately.
  • As her partner: check in often, make it safe to say "I'm struggling." Postpartum depression affects all parents — you too.
Nutrition & hydration for mom
  • Breastfeeding = ~500 extra calories/day needed. Do not restrict food.
  • Drink water constantly — at least 8–10 glasses/day. Keep a large bottle at every feeding spot.
  • Continue prenatal vitamins while breastfeeding
  • Iron-rich foods help recovery: leafy greens, lean meat, lentils, fortified cereals
  • Caffeine: limit to ~200mg/day (about one 12oz coffee)
  • Alcohol: wait 2 hours per drink before breastfeeding, or pump and dump
Activity & rest
  • No strenuous exercise until 6-week OB clearance (8–12 weeks for c-section)
  • Gentle walks are encouraged once comfortable
  • No sex until 6-week OB clearance
  • No baths or swimming until postpartum bleeding has stopped
  • Pelvic floor exercises (Kegels) can begin right away
  • Sleep when baby sleeps — protect her rest above almost everything else
Tips & tricks
Feeding tips
1
The "dream feed"Feed baby right before you go to sleep (10–11pm) without fully waking them. Can extend the first stretch of nighttime sleep.
2
Skin-to-skin for latch troubleStrip both mom and baby down — contact stimulates feeding instincts and helps with latch.
3
Football hold for engorgementTuck baby under your arm for better latch control when breasts are very full.
4
Hand-express before latchingIf very engorged, express a little first to soften the areola so baby can latch more easily.
Sleep tips
1
White noise louder than you thinkThe womb is as loud as a vacuum cleaner. A white noise machine at ~65 decibels can be transformative.
2
Catch the sleep windowPut baby down at the first yawn or eye-rub. An overtired baby fights sleep hard.
3
Warm the bassinet sheetA heating pad on the sheet for a few minutes before transfer (remove first!) prevents the cold-sheet startle wake.
4
Hold upright after feedsKeep baby upright for 15–20 minutes before putting down. Reduces spit-up and nighttime discomfort.
Survival tips for parents
1
Set up feeding stationsSnacks, water bottle, phone charger, burp cloth, remote — at every place you regularly feed. You'll be there a lot.
2
Accept every offer of helpWhen someone asks, say "Bring a meal" or "Come hold the baby while I shower." Specific asks get results.
3
Double-diaper at nightPut two diapers on at bedtime to absorb more and reduce the chance of a 3am blowout.
4
Onesie snaps go down, not upThe envelope fold at the neck lets you pull a blowout outfit down off baby instead of over their head.
5
It's okay to put baby downIf overwhelmed, put baby in their safe sleep space and step away for a few minutes. A crying baby in a safe place is okay.
Late preterm baby tips (37 weeks)
  • Late preterm babies are often sleepier — wake them to feed every 2–3 hours. Don't let them sleep through feeds.
  • They tire at the breast faster — shorter, more frequent feeds are normal.
  • More prone to jaundice — watch for yellowing of skin and eyes, especially days 3–5.
  • May take longer to establish breastfeeding — a lactation consultant is your best tool.
  • First pediatrician visit: 2–3 days after discharge. Book before you leave the hospital.

When to call / seek help
Call 911 or go to the ER immediately if: baby stops breathing, turns blue/purple around mouth, is unresponsive or limp, has a seizure, or has a major injury.
Call the pediatrician immediately if: fever of 100.4°F / 38°C or higher in a baby under 3 months, temperature below 97°F, baby not waking to feed after 4+ hours, fewer than 6 wet diapers after day 4, blood in stool, jaundice deepening, persistent inconsolable crying, difficulty breathing, or umbilical cord area is red, swollen, or has foul discharge.
Call during business hours if: forceful/consistent vomiting, no bowel movement for 5+ days, spreading rash, white patches in mouth (thrush), or signs of mastitis.
Call OB for mom if: heavy bleeding (soaking a pad in under an hour), large clots, fever over 100.4°F, foul-smelling discharge, incision site separating or infected, inability to urinate, or symptoms of postpartum depression or anxiety.
Appointments to book: Pediatrician at 2–3 days post-discharge (late preterm), 2 weeks, and 1 month. OB for mom at 6 weeks (or 2 weeks for c-section). Book before leaving the hospital.
